Celebrating a Quarter Century in Space

The International Space Station (ISS) as a beacon of human collaboration, scientific innovation, and continuous exploration.

A Journey of Collaboration

November 20, 1998

The Beginning: Zarya Module

The first module, Zarya, is launched from Russia, marking the start of the ISS construction. This module provided the initial propulsion and power.

December 1998

First Connection: Unity Module

The US Unity module (Node 1) is launched and attached to Zarya, symbolizing the first major international collaboration on the project.

November 2, 2000

Arrival of the First Crew

Expedition 1 arrives at the station. Human presence on the ISS has been uninterrupted ever since.

2009

Expansion and Assembly Completion

The main assembly of the station is completed. The crew is expanded to six members, allowing a significant increase in scientific research.

The Orbiting Laboratory

Biology & Biotechnology

Studies on cell growth and the effects of microgravity on the human body, leading to advances in medicine on Earth.

Materials Science

Research in microgravity allows for the creation of new materials with unique properties, impossible to replicate on Earth.

Earth Observation

The ISS is a unique platform for monitoring Earth's climate, natural disasters, and environmental changes, providing crucial data.

The Next 25 Years and Beyond

The Bridge to Deep Space Exploration

The ISS serves as an essential testbed for technologies needed for future missions to the Moon, Mars, and beyond.

New Commercial Partnerships

The future of low Earth orbit includes growing collaboration with private companies, opening new opportunities for research and tourism.
